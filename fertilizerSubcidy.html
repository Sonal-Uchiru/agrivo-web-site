Project Scope

Background Literature

Paddy cultivation, a fundamental aspect of Sri Lanka's agriculture and cultural heritage, has a rich history spanning over two millennia. It has played a significant role in shaping the nation's socio-economic fabric and is closely tied to the lives of its people. Rice, the staple food in Sri Lanka, symbolizes self-sufficiency and is deeply ingrained in the country's history. Over time, paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka has undergone notable transformations, from ancient hydraulic systems to modern mechanized farming methods. These changes have been influenced by technology, environmental factors, and socio-economic shifts, which are critical to understanding the present state of rice farming in the country. Today, paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka faces several challenges, including climate change, land disputes, and changing dietary preferences. These issues have direct implications for rice production. Additionally, the country's integration into global markets and economic development efforts have led to shifts in agricultural policies and practices. Nutrient management is crucial for successful paddy cultivation, with rice crops requiring various nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulfur, calcium, magnesium, iron, zinc, and manganese. Proper fertilizer management is essential to achieve high yields, as overuse can lead to environmental issues, while inadequate application can result in lower yields and nutrient deficiencies.

Sri Lanka has implemented a fertilizer subsidy program, which aims to reduce the cost of fertilizers and promote their use to enhance agricultural output. The government provides four types of fertilizers to farmers, some for free and others at a discounted rate. While the program has contributed to the growth of the agriculture sector and improved food security, it has also faced criticisms related to issues like corruption, leakage, and unsustainable fertilizer usage. Efficient fertilizer application, following the 4Rs framework (correct source, amount, timing, and placement of nutrients), is essential to maximize the effectiveness of fertilizers. Proper timing of fertilization is crucial to minimize nutrient losses and enhance plant uptake. The current distribution of fertilizer subsidies in Sri Lanka is a manual process, involving farmer registration, field inspections, and token distribution. This system operates on a first-come-first-served basis, and there have been calls for reforms to address issues in the distribution process.

Research Gap

Research A

Examines the Fertilizer Subsidy Program in Sri Lanka, explaining its structure and issues. The authors suggest policy options for the program while considering economic, environmental, and socio-political factors. They mention that fertilizer subsidies in developing countries can lead to misuse, corruption, and dependency. The government has options to continue the current policy, fully remove the subsidy, or reform it by supporting target groups at a minimal cost. The government can also combine these options into different policy packages, such as targeting deserving farmers, using vouchers, or gradually reducing the subsidy rate.

Research B

H. K. J. Ekanayake has done an in-depth analysis of the impact of the fertilizer subsidy on paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka by using separate demand functions along with a simple regression model. The research found that fertilizer demand functions could vary according to the type of fertilizer, but in general, fertilizer demand is not significantly affected by changes in its own price, output price, and related policies in the short run. Farmers tend to apply fertilizer despite difficulties to ensure optimum yield levels. However, an increase in fertilizer prices could reduce farmers' profits by increasing the cost of production. Increasing paddy prices or decreasing fertilizer prices are alternatives available to the government to provide incentives to paddy farmers in the short run, but policymakers should weigh the pros and cons of each option. A well-targeted subsidy scheme, which is gradually phased out, is preferred in the short run, and public expenditure should focus on providing infrastructure and institutional facilities required to improve efficiency in paddy marketing to allow paddy prices to be attractive. Once productivity and profitability in paddy cultivation are increased to an acceptable level, fertilizer subsidy could be withdrawn completely.

Research C

The study examines the impact of fertilizer use on paddy production in Sri Lanka from 1962 to 2020 using the ARDL-ECM approach. Results show that fertilizer use and cultivation extent have positive and statistically significant elasticities in both the short and long run. Fertilizer contributes 35% to output growth and has a positive effect on productivity, although its contribution has declined over time. Restricting fertilizer supply can negatively affect rice production. Therefore, there is a need for more sustainable fertilizer policies that take into account market implications domestically and internationally. Policies related to land, fertilizer, or other fundamental inputs require experimentation for understanding and should be accorded time for the effects to be realized.

The three analyzed research articles explore the impact of the fertilizer subsidy program on paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka. The articles suggest policy changes, like gradually getting rid of the subsidies and using fertilizer in a more sustainable way. However, they didn't explain how to actually make these changes happen in Sri Lanka's current situation. In addition, they didn’t address the challenges of applying fertilizer in a timely manner to the paddy fields.

Research Problems

In addition to research gap analysis, Interviews were conducted with small-scale farmers and resource persons from agriculture centers in order to identify the problems related to the fertilizer subsidy program. The results revealed that a crucial problem that plagues the fertilizer subsidy program which needs immediate attention is the absence of a priority-based fertilizer distribution process.

Farmers not receiving fertilizer subsidy

The current first-come, first-serve distribution process of fertilizer subsidy in Sri Lanka results in farmers with a more urgent need for fertilizers not receiving the subsidy. This can lead to lower crop yields and financial losses for these farmers.

Disorganized and inefficient distribution process

The lack of a priority-based selection system in the fertilizer subsidy program of Sri Lanka results in a disorganized and inefficient distribution process. This leads to long queues on distribution days, leading to a waste of time for farmers and agricultural officers.

Difficulties in ensuring the timely and proper application of fertilizers

The absence of a priority-based selection process makes it difficult to ensure the timely and proper application of fertilizers to the paddy fields. This can result in sub-optimal crop yields, negatively impacting the agricultural sector and overall economy of Sri Lanka.

Negative impact on agriculture and economy

The problems caused by the absence of a priority-based distribution process for fertilizer subsidy can have a negative impact on the agricultural sector and the overall economy of Sri Lanka.

Proposed Solution

The "Priority Distribution and Smart Scheduling Model" introduced in this research component serves as a comprehensive solution designed to optimize the allocation of fertilizer subsidies to paddy farmers while considering the urgency of their needs. This multifaceted approach leverages advanced machine learning algorithms and incorporates a notification component for efficient distribution. The research findings highlight key outcomes:

1. Utilization of Advanced Algorithms

The research incorporates machine learning algorithms, including Decision Trees, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Support Vector Machine, to predict priority scores based on farmers' details. This implementation is a novel approach to prioritize subsidy distribution, taking into account various factors contributing to the priority score.

2. Data Preprocessing and Imbalance Handling

Rigorous data preprocessing techniques, including data cleaning, transformation, and feature selection, were applied to ensure data quality and reliability. The study addressed data imbalances using oversampling, under-sampling, and class weight techniques, enhancing the accuracy of prediction models.

3. Evaluation of Machine Learning Models

Multiple supervised learning models were evaluated using performance metrics such as precision, recall, F1 score, and overall accuracy. The research identifies the best-performing model, which plays a crucial role in generating a priority distribution list for farmers.

4. Notification Component

The notification component is a pivotal part of the research model, enabling the efficient distribution of allocated time slots to registered farmers. This component facilitates timely and convenient communication with farmers regarding their fertilizer subsidy collection, ensuring a smooth and organized process.

Research Objectives

Collect and analyze datasets on fertilizer utilization and distribution

This objective involves gathering data on fertilizer usage and distribution in the target area. The collected data will be analyzed to identify patterns and trends that can be used to inform the development of the priority distribution schedule.

Conduct a survey among farmers and officers to determine parameters for the priority schedule

This objective involves conducting a survey among farmers and officers to gather information on factors that should be taken into consideration when developing the priority schedule. The survey will help identify the parameters that should be used to rank farmers and determine the priority distribution schedule.

Implement multiple algorithms and select the best-performing one based on a performance index

This objective involves implementing multiple algorithms for generating the priority distribution schedule. The performance of each algorithm will be evaluated based on a set of predetermined metrics, and the best-performing algorithm will be selected for further development.

Develop an ML model to generate the priority distribution schedule

This objective involves developing a machine learning model that can generate the priority distribution schedule based on the parameters identified in objective 2. The model will be trained on the data collected in Objective 1 and will use the best-performing algorithm identified in Objective 3.

Compare the performance of the ML model and the best-performing algorithm and choose the optimal solution provider

This objective involves comparing the performance of the ML model developed in objective 4 with the best-performing algorithm identified in objective 3. The comparison will be based on a set of predetermined metrics, and the optimal solution provider will be selected.

Create a time slot schedule and notify farmers using SMS notifications

This objective involves creating a schedule for fertilizer distribution based on the priority distribution schedule developed in objective 5. The schedule will be communicated to farmers using SMS notifications, which they can scan using their mobile phones to access information about their scheduled time slot for fertilizer distribution.

Methodology

The methodology employed in this study is characterized by a systematic approach aimed at enhancing the efficiency of fertilizer distribution within the agricultural landscape of Sri Lanka. This methodological framework has been meticulously structured to address the overarching objective of optimizing fertilizer accessibility and effectiveness for the agricultural community.

The system diagram depicted in the figure gives significant attention to the fertilizer subcidy component and its intricate connections with various system elements. The priority distribution system operates as a web application primarily managed by Agricultural Instructors (AIs). The system employs a backend Flask server with various components: Controller for request management, Service for data processing, Priority Score Prediction for equitable subsidy allocation, Time Slot Generation for optimizing distribution, and Notification for SMS-based farmer communication. This web-based system streamlines subsidy distribution and prioritizes efficiency, employing intricate interactions within its multifaceted backend architecture.

1. Data Collection, Pre-Processing and Analysis

The initial phase of this methodology centers on the exhaustive collection and subsequent analysis of data pertaining to fertilizer utilization and distribution within the targeted geographical area. This foundational dataset serves as the linchpin upon which subsequent actions are premised. Through a rigorous process of data pre-processing, analysis, discernible patterns and trends germane to fertilizer usage and distribution are identified. This analytical endeavor is of paramount importance, serving to inform the development of a priority distribution schedule.

2. Stakeholder Engagement through Surveys

The second salient stage of this methodology entails proactive engagement with key stakeholders, specifically farmers and agricultural officers, through structured surveys. The purpose of these surveys is to elicit valuable insights, perspectives, and considerations that should guide the formulation of the priority distribution schedule. The input obtained from these critical stakeholders plays an instrumental role in the stratification of farmers and the delineation of criteria governing the priority distribution schedule.

3. Algorithm Evaluation

With the aim of optimizing the fertilizer distribution process, this methodology proceeds to the implementation of multiple algorithms for the generation of the priority distribution schedule. The selection of the most efficacious algorithm hinges upon a rigorous evaluation of performance, benchmarked against predetermined metrics. This objective-driven evaluation is pivotal in the identification of the algorithm that exhibits the highest degree of efficiency, thereby informing subsequent developmental efforts.

4. Machine Learning Model Development

Capitalizing on contemporary advancements in machine learning, this methodology advances to the development of a sophisticated machine learning model. This model leverages the wealth of data amassed and integrates the algorithm identified as the most performant. Notably, this machine learning model is engineered with adaptability as a core feature, capable of evolving in response to changing contextual variables, rendering it a resilient solution for the optimization of fertilizer distribution.

5. Performance Enhancement

At the heart of this methodology lies the rigorous evaluation of performance. In this phase, an exhaustive comparative analysis is conducted to further enhance the performance machine learning model crafted. This comparative assessment is firmly grounded in a predetermined set of metrics, culminating in the selection of the optimal solution provider for fertilizer distribution.

6. Implementation of timeslot distribution and notification mechanism

Recognizing the significance of user-friendliness and contemporary communication modalities, the final phase of this methodology involves the creation of a structured time slot schedule for fertilizer distribution. Derived from the priority distribution schedule developed, this schedule is disseminated to farmers through the innovative medium of SMS notifications. This intuitive approach facilitates real-time access to distribution schedules via mobile devices, augmenting accessibility and convenience for farmers.

This methodological framework has been systematically designed to revolutionize the distribution of fertilizers in Sri Lanka. It deploys data-driven insights, actively engages key stakeholders, harnesses cutting-edge technology, and prioritizes user-centric design principles. Adhering to this structured approach, the aspiration is to optimize the fertilizer distribution process, effectively address challenges within the agricultural sector, and contribute substantively to the sustainable advancement of agriculture in Sri Lanka.
