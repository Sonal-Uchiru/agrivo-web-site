Project Scope

Background Literature

Sri Lanka's agricultural calendar is significantly shaped by two predominant monsoon winds, the southwest monsoon from May to September and the northeast monsoon from November to February. These monsoons give rise to two distinct seasons in the country's agricultural landscape:

1. Maha Season:

● The primary period for paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka.

● In the northern and eastern regions, it spans from October to January, while in the southern and western parts, it extends from April to July.

● This season plays a crucial role in the country's agricultural production, particularly for paddy crops.

2. Yala Season:

● Also known as the "Bala season," it serves as the secondary paddy growing season.

● Occurs from May to September in the northern and eastern regions and from November to February in the southern and western areas.

● Although important for agricultural continuity, the Yala season typically yields a lower paddy output compared to the Maha season.

● These two seasons reflect the influence of monsoons and highlight the strategic nature of agricultural practices in Sri Lanka. Farmers align their crop cycles with these seasonal shifts to ensure food security and sustain the nation's rich agricultural heritage.

Traditional methods of weather prediction have played a vital role in Sri Lanka's paddy cultivation for over two millennia. Ancient texts and oral traditions have preserved the wisdom of anticipating weather and its impact on agriculture. These methods incorporate observations of celestial body movements, seasonal transitions, animal behavior, and insect activity. In modern times, traditional prediction methods have merged with scientific techniques, including meteorological data and satellite imagery. However, traditional methods continue to be essential, especially in rural areas where access to modern information is limited. While traditional prediction methods have advantages, they also have limitations, including limited accuracy due to subjective observations, inflexibility in adapting to changing conditions, reliance on folklore and superstitions, labor-intensive practices, limited accessibility, and challenges in addressing the impacts of climate change. Balancing traditional wisdom with modern scientific knowledge is essential for ensuring the sustainability and resilience of Sri Lanka's agriculture.

Research A

In 2021, researchers Piyal Ekanayake, Windhya Rankothge, Rukmal Weliwatta, and Jeevani W. Jayasinghe conducted a study on crop-weather models for paddy yield in Sri Lanka, focusing on various weather indices such as temperature, rainfall, and sunshine hours. The key areas addressed in their research include:

1. Enhancing Predictive Accuracy: The research aimed to develop more accurate predictive models for paddy yield by utilizing a comprehensive set of weather indices. This is crucial in Sri Lanka, where the monsoon climate and weather variability significantly affect agricultural outcomes.

2. Climate Resilience: The study is part of broader efforts to make Sri Lankan agriculture more climate-resilient. By identifying how different weather factors impact paddy yield, farmers can better adapt to changing climatic conditions and mitigate potential risks.

3. Precision Agriculture: The findings can be applied in the practice of precision agriculture. Farmers can use weather data to fine-tune their cultivation practices, leading to more efficient and sustainable farming, including optimized planting and harvesting times, irrigation scheduling, and fertilizer and pesticide use.

4. Data-Driven Decision-Making: The research emphasizes the importance of data-driven decision-making in agriculture. The models developed can be used by farmers and policymakers to make informed choices regarding crop management, resource allocation, and disaster preparedness.

5. Economic Benefits: Improving paddy yield prediction can lead to economic benefits for farmers. By optimizing resource utilization and minimizing crop losses due to adverse weather conditions, farmers can increase their income and financial stability.

The study used statistics from seven geographical regions that contribute to about two-thirds of Sri Lanka's total paddy production. They applied five regression techniques to model linear and non-linear relationships, with the Random Forest (RF) model proving to be the most accurate. The RF model demonstrated a high Coefficient Correlation (R) of 0.99 and the lowest Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) of 1.4%. In conclusion, the research conducted by Ekanayake, Rankothge, Weliwatta, and Jayasinghe in 2021 represents a significant step toward making Sri Lankan agriculture more resilient, sustainable, and efficient in the face of changing weather patterns and climate uncertainties. This research has the potential to benefit both farmers and society as a whole by ensuring food security and environmental sustainability.

Research B

In 2020 researchers named Vinushi Amarathunge, Lasini Wickramasinghe, Anushka Perera, Jeewani Jayasinghe and Upeka Rathnayake conducted research to obtain the relationships between paddy yield and climatic parameters for several paddy-grown areas in Sri Lanka namely Ampara, Batticaloa, Badulla, Bandarawela, Hambantota, Trincomalee, Kurunegala, and Puttalam. This paper points out the following key features.

1. Regional Variations: One of the significant aspects of this research is its focus on multiple paddy-growing regions in Sri Lanka, including Ampara, Batticaloa, Badulla, Bandarawela, Hambantota, Trincomalee, Kurunegala, and Puttalam. Examining different regions allows for a comprehensive understanding of how local climatic variations impact paddy yield. This information is crucial for tailoring agricultural strategies to specific geographic conditions.

2. Climate Change Adaptation: Understanding the relationships between climatic parameters and paddy yield is particularly relevant in the context of climate change. Sri Lanka, like many other countries, faces shifting weather patterns and increased climate variability. The research findings can inform adaptive strategies to help farmers cope with these changes, ensuring food security.

3. Resource Optimization: The research findings may facilitate the optimization of resources such as water and fertilizers. Farmers can use climate data to determine when and how much irrigation is needed and adjust fertilizer application based on expected weather conditions, reducing waste and environmental impact.

Artificial Neural Networks(ANN) was used to obtain the relationship between climatic variables and yield and predict yield estimation. Three training algorithms were used to train the developed ANN model. Among the three algorithms, results show that Levenberg–Marquardt (LM) has outperformed the other two in determining the relationship between climatic factors and paddy yield with less computational time. In summary, the research holds significant importance for Sri Lanka's agricultural sector. It provides valuable insights into how climatic parameters impact paddy yield in different regions, empowering farmers, policymakers, and researchers to make informed decisions aimed at increasing food security and adapting to a changing climate.

While these research studies focus on climate variables, they often overlook the effect of non-climatic factors like soil qualities. Furthermore, while various forecasting apps are available, most do not provide solutions that account for both climatic and non-climatic factors influencing yield. The few that do exist tend to be costly or impractical for the majority of Sri Lanka's small-scale farmers. Because of that many rural farmers continue to rely on less effective traditional methods.

Research Problems

Inaccurate paddy yield estimations indeed present significant challenges for farmers and the agricultural industry in Sri Lanka. These issues can be considered as research problems with broad implications for both agricultural sustainability and the livelihoods of farming communities.

Difficulty in Planning and Decision-Making

● Research Problem: Inaccurate yield predictions hinder farmers' ability to make informed decisions regarding planting schedules, harvesting times, and marketing strategies.

● Implications: This leads to suboptimal resource allocation, potential crop losses due to unfavorable weather conditions, and difficulties in managing finances and labor effectively.

Climate Change Vulnerability

● Research Problem: Inaccurate yield estimations exacerbate farmers' vulnerability to the impacts of climate change, including rising temperatures, altered rainfall patterns, and extreme weather events.

● Implications: Farmers may struggle to adapt to changing conditions, resulting in reduced crop yields, increased production costs, and food security risks for both farming households and the broader population.

Lack of Crop Insurance Access

● Research Problem: Small-scale farmers often lack access to crop insurance, making them particularly vulnerable to crop failures caused by inaccurate yield predictions.

● Implications: Without a safety net, farmers may experience financial instability and be unable to recover from significant crop losses, leading to increased poverty and dependence on subsistence farming.

Technology and Data Gaps

● Research Problem: Gaps in technology and data availability may contribute to inaccurate yield predictions.

● Implications: Addressing these gaps through research and technological advancements is essential for improving the accuracy of yield predictions and ensuring that farmers have access to the necessary tools and information.

In conclusion, addressing the research problems associated with inaccurate paddy yield estimations in Sri Lanka is essential for the sustainability of the agricultural sector, the livelihoods of farming communities, and the overall food security of the nation. Researchers, policymakers, and stakeholders should collaborate to develop reliable predictive models and provide support systems that enable farmers to cope with the challenges posed by climate change and inaccurate yield predictions. Apart from that, a pressing concern among farmers in Sri Lanka revolves around their limited access to advanced predictive systems and their insufficient knowledge to effectively utilize these technologies. Many farmers remain unaware of the existence and potential advantages of such systems, creating a substantial knowledge gap in the agricultural sector. Compounding this issue is the financial constraint faced by a significant portion of these farmers, which often prevents them from investing in and adopting modern agricultural technologies. As a result, they are left without the means to use advanced predictive systems, thereby hindering their ability to make informed decisions and optimize resource management. This problem not only places their agricultural endeavors at risk but also delays their capacity to adapt to the growing challenges posed by changing climatic conditions, ultimately jeopardizing food security and the overall sustainability of agriculture in Sri Lanka. Addressing this issue emerges as a critical research problem that necessitates innovative solutions to bridge the technology and knowledge gap, ensuring the resilience and prosperity of Sri Lankan farming communities. Moreover, it highlights the need for comprehensive educational programs to empower farmers with the tools and knowledge required to effectively leverage predictive systems for better crop management and sustainable agricultural practices. In conclusion, bridging the gap in access to technology and knowledge among Sri Lankan farmers is not only imperative for their livelihoods but also essential for improving the country's agricultural sector in the face of evolving environmental challenges.

Proposed Solution

Extensive research findings have shed light on the prevailing agricultural practices in Sri Lanka, revealing that a substantial portion, approximately 43% of the current population, continues to rely on traditional forecasting techniques for their farming endeavors. Alarmingly, an additional 40% of the population remains unaware of the potential alignment between traditional knowledge and modern weather reports. This dual challenge of traditionalism and limited awareness has significant ramifications, including resource allocation inefficiencies and financial hardships among the farming community. In response to these pressing issues, our research endeavors culminated in the development of an innovative and technologically-driven solution: a mobile application designed to bridge the gap between traditional farming practices and modern precision agriculture. This application serves as a beacon of hope for small-scale paddy farmers, who are undeniably the lifeblood of Sri Lanka's economy. It offers them not only an opportunity to embrace technological solutions beyond their time-honored traditions but also provides access to an accurate yield prediction system that considers both climatic and non-climatic factors. The main beneficiaries of this innovative application are the hardworking small-scale paddy farmers in Sri Lanka, and its deployment has the potential to usher in a new era of agricultural sustainability and economic prosperity for this vital demographic. To improve the model's performance preprocessing and feature engineering techniques were used. Through careful data cleaning, transformation, and selection, we were able to extract and emphasize the most pertinent features, thereby reducing noise and increasing the model's predictive power. Moreover, the incorporation of a novel "soil points" system, which evaluates the compatibility of soil qualities with preferred paddy types, played a crucial role in feature engineering, allowing for more precise yield estimations. In addition, increasing the size of the datasets also improved the model accuracy. In summary, these research findings will pave the way for a brighter future for Sri Lanka's small-scale paddy farmers, offering them technological solutions and accurate yield predictions while addressing traditionalism and awareness challenges in the agricultural sector.

Research Objectives

Identifying Influential Variables

1. Rationale: Recognizing these variables is fundamental for building a robust yield prediction model. Climatic variables like rainfall, relative humidity, temperature, wind speed, sunshine hours, and non-climatic factors such as soil fertility and texture play pivotal roles in crop growth.

2. Interconnected Factors: It's crucial to acknowledge that these variables often influence each other in many ways. For instance, elevated humidity levels can foster increased pest activity, posing a threat to crops. Meanwhile, excessive rainfall may result in soil erosion, nutrient loss, and reduced soil fertility due to the washing away of topsoil layers.

Identifying the Connections Between Variables

1. Data Collection: This process initiates with comprehensive data collection, encompassing historical climatic data, soil types, and their qualities across different regions, as well as historical data pertaining to paddy production in diverse areas.

2. Data Analysis: Following data collection, a detailed analysis will be carried out to unveil the relationships and correlations between climatic and non-climatic variables. For example, it seeks to reveal how variations in rainfall patterns affect soil quality, with a focus on understanding the consequences of excess rainfall, such as soil erosion, and the impact of inadequate rainfall, leading to soil dryness and reduced soil fertility.

Analyzing and Understanding Effective Yield Prediction Models

1. Purpose and Scope: This includes determining the purpose and scope of these models, specifying the variables employed for yield prediction, and the performance metrics used to evaluate these models.

2. Accuracy and Precision Assessment: A critical component involves analyzing the precision and accuracy of these models in predicting paddy yield across diverse environmental conditions and geographic areas.

3. Limitations Identification: Identifying the limitations of existing models, including their applicability within certain environmental conditions, is essential for refining and improving the predictive capabilities of the selected model.

Implementing the Selected Crop Model

1. Programming Language Selection: This stage begins by selecting the most appropriate programming language that aligns with the model's requirements and compatibility.

2. Data Loading: Loading and organizing data in the most suitable format, considering potential variations in input data types and formats that may be required by different models.

3. Data Preprocessing: Data preprocessing tasks, such as data cleaning, feature engineering, and data scaling, will be performed to prepare the data for model training.

4. Model Training: The selected model will be trained using preprocessed data, with an emphasis on optimizing model parameters and settings for accurate yield predictions.

5. Evaluation and Fine-Tuning: Based on the results of model evaluation, fine-tuning may be necessary. This may involve parameter adjustments, inclusion of additional variables, or model refinements to enhance predictive accuracy.

Methodology

The system diagram illustrates paddy yield prediction methodology, involving key components: the Node Server ensures secure data storage, the Flask Server processes user input and yields accurate forecasts, the Climate Prediction Component forecasts future climate conditions, and the Yield Prediction Component calculates crop yields with precision. Users interact through a user-friendly web interface to provide input, and the system delivers vital information, empowering farmers to optimize their paddy cultivation practices.

This methodology is the blueprint for empowering small-scale farmers in Sri Lanka through an innovative paddy yield prediction system. This approach outlines the systematic process we follow to create a mobile-based solution for farmers' needs. At its core, our methodology revolves around two distinct prediction approaches: district-wise and GN division-wise predictions. These approaches are seamlessly integrated to provide farmers with tailored insights for better decision-making. The first step is the data collection, where crucial information is gathered to fuel the system. This encompasses everything from climate data to soil assessments and historical crop yield records. Next, the main focus is on the core of this methodology: model development. This phase involves creating a predictive model that considers various variables, including climate and soil conditions. Once the model is in place, the prediction phase begins. Here, the integrated data is utilized to provide farmers with accurate forecasts of their crop yields. This information equips them to plan cultivating and harvesting schedules, make sensible choices regarding crop varieties, and optimize resource usage. User-friendliness is paramount in the methodology, and thus, system pays close attention to designing an intuitive user interface that simplifies data input and system interaction. Apart from that Validation plays a crucial role as well. Continuous assessment of the model’s accuracy is conducted by comparing its predictions with actual yield outcomes, ensuring its dependability and effectiveness. In essence, this methodology guides this quest to address the challenges faced by small-scale farmers while contributing to the sustainability and resilience of agriculture in Sri Lanka.
